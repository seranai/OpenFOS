# Beachhead Scoring Framework

## Detailed Rubric for the 7-Dimension Beachhead Scorecard

This reference file contains the detailed scoring rubric used in Phase 3 of the Market &
Beachhead Selection skill. Read this before producing the Full Beachhead Scorecard.

The 7 dimensions are derived from three foundational frameworks:
- **Bill Aulet's Disciplined Entrepreneurship** (MIT): Market segmentation criteria and
  the three conditions that define a beachhead market
- **Geoffrey Moore's Crossing the Chasm**: Bowling pin strategy and the importance of
  word-of-mouth within segments
- **Steve Blank's Customer Development**: Reachability and founder-market fit as
  go-to-market accelerants

---

## Dimension 1: Pain Urgency (Is this segment on fire?)

The core question: How badly does this specific segment need the solution, and how urgently
are they looking for it RIGHT NOW?

This is distinct from problem severity (scored in Skill 1). Here we're asking: within this
specific segment, how acute is the pain compared to other segments?

| Score | Description | Evidence Markers |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| 9-10  | Regulatory deadline, compliance risk, or measurable revenue loss happening NOW. | Active RFPs in this segment. Buyers calling vendors. Budget already allocated. Recent regulatory change creating urgency (e.g., GDPR created urgent pain for EU data handlers). |
| 7-8   | Significant pain with trigger events happening regularly. | Segment has seasonal or cyclical triggers. Companies in this segment are actively hiring to solve the problem manually. Trade publications writing about this pain point. |
| 5-6   | Acknowledged pain but no forcing function. | Buyers in this segment nod when you describe the problem but aren't actively searching for solutions. "Yes, that's a pain, but we're managing." |
| 3-4   | Low-priority pain. | The problem exists but is buried under higher priorities. Would need significant education to create urgency. |
| 1-2   | Minimal or unrecognized pain in this segment. | This segment doesn't experience the problem as acutely as others, or has adapted so well that pain has receded. |

**Scoring guidance:** Look for trigger events. A segment with a clear trigger event
(regulatory change, seasonal cycle, growth milestone, technology migration) scores higher
than one with chronic-but-manageable pain. Trigger events create buying urgency.

**Data to search for:** Recent regulatory changes affecting the segment, industry reports
on top pain points, job postings that signal manual workarounds, conference session topics
that indicate active problem-solving.

## Dimension 2: Willingness to Pay (Will this segment open wallets?)

The core question: Does this segment have both the budget authority and the economic
incentive to pay for a solution at your target price point?

| Score | Description | Evidence Markers |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| 9-10  | Segment has established budget categories for this type of solution. Clear ROI math works. | Competitors successfully selling to this segment at similar or higher price points. Economic buyer is clearly identifiable. ROI calculation is straightforward: "saves X hours/week at $Y/hour." |
| 7-8   | Budget exists but may be in adjacent categories. Strong economic logic. | Segment is paying for partial solutions (consulting, manual labor, point tools). Budget would shift from existing spend, not require new budget creation. |
| 5-6   | Economic logic exists but budget creation required. | Segment doesn't currently allocate budget to this problem category. Would need to build a business case. Decision requires VP+ approval. |
| 3-4   | Weak economic justification at target price point. | Segment is price-sensitive. Free or open-source alternatives are "good enough." Small company size means every dollar is scrutinized. |
| 1-2   | No apparent willingness or ability to pay. | Segment expects free solutions. No budget authority for this category. Economic buyer doesn't exist. |

**B2B price point calibration:**

| Company Size | Reasonable Monthly SaaS Spend | Decision Process |
|--------------|-------------------------------|-------------------|
| 1-10 employees | $50-200/month | Founder decides in minutes |
| 11-50 employees | $200-1,000/month | Manager + founder, days to weeks |
| 51-200 employees | $1,000-5,000/month | VP approval, weeks to months |
| 201-1,000 employees | $5,000-25,000/month | Committee, months |
| 1,000+ employees | $25,000+/month | Enterprise procurement, 3-12 months |

**Critical cross-check:** Match the founder's target ACV against the segment's buying
capacity. A $2,000/month tool targeting 5-person startups will fail regardless of how
strong the pain is.

**Counter-evidence to consider:** Even in segments with high WTP, long procurement cycles
can kill a bootstrapped startup. Enterprise willingness to pay $100K/year is meaningless
if the sales cycle is 9 months and the founder has 6 months of runway. Score this
dimension in context of the founder's financial situation.

## Dimension 3: Market Accessibility (Can you actually reach these buyers?)

The core question: Can the founder get in front of 50 potential buyers in this segment
within 30 days?

| Score | Description | Evidence Markers |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| 9-10  | Highly concentrated with clear channels. Buyers self-identify. | Active online communities (Slack, Discord, forums). Regular industry events. Specific LinkedIn filters identify exact buyers. Niche publications reach the audience. Founder has warm introductions to 10+. |
| 7-8   | Identifiable and reachable with focused effort. | Can build a target list from public data. LinkedIn outbound works for this segment. Content marketing channels exist. 1-2 conferences per year reach the audience. |
| 5-6   | Reachable but dispersed. No single channel dominates. | Buyers are spread across industries or geographies. Multiple channels needed. Requires more creative outreach strategies. |
| 3-4   | Fragmented and hard to identify. | Small businesses with low digital footprint. No industry associations. No community gathering points. High CAC expected. |
| 1-2   | Practically unreachable at startup scale. | No clear way to identify buyers. Regulatory barriers to outreach. Market operates through relationships that take years to build. |

**Channel inventory checklist** (score higher if multiple channels exist):
- [ ] LinkedIn: Can you build a saved search that returns 500+ prospects?
- [ ] Communities: Are there Slack/Discord/Facebook groups with 1,000+ members?
- [ ] Events: Are there 2+ annual conferences specifically for this segment?
- [ ] Content: Are there newsletters, podcasts, or publications this segment follows?
- [ ] Associations: Is there an industry association with a member directory?
- [ ] Referral networks: Do buyers in this segment actively recommend tools to peers?
- [ ] Partner channels: Are there consultants, agencies, or integrators serving this segment?

**Emerging market modifiers:**
- Strong WhatsApp/Telegram community culture → add 1 point (alternative channel)
- Low credit card penetration → subtract 1 point (payment friction affects accessibility)
- Relationship-based selling culture → subtract 1 point for outbound approaches,
  add 1 point if founder has existing network
- Government/institutional buyers → subtract 2 points (procurement complexity)

## Dimension 4: Competitive Openness (Is there room to win?)

The core question: How easy is it to displace or circumvent existing solutions in this
segment?

| Score | Description | Evidence Markers |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| 9-10  | Green field. No dedicated solution exists. | Segment is using spreadsheets, email, or manual processes. No funded competitor is specifically targeting this segment. Customers have no vendor loyalty to break. |
| 7-8   | Existing solutions are generic, not segment-specific. | Horizontal tools are being used (Salesforce, HubSpot, Notion, Excel) but no one has built a segment-specific solution. Customers are frustrated with workarounds. |
| 5-6   | Competition exists but has clear weaknesses. | 1-2 competitors serve this segment but have known limitations (poor UX, missing features, wrong pricing, bad support). Switching costs are moderate. |
| 3-4   | Entrenched competition. | Well-funded competitors with strong brand in this segment. High switching costs. Customers are generally satisfied. |
| 1-2   | Dominant incumbent with deep moat. | A single player owns 50%+ of the segment. Deep integrations make switching painful. Customers have multi-year contracts. |

**Two-sided analysis (present both):**

**The "greenfield is good" argument:** No competition means no one has educated the buyer
or proven the category. You own the narrative. You set the standard. First-mover advantage
in a specific niche can be powerful.

**The "greenfield is dangerous" counter-argument:** No competition might mean no market.
If nobody has built this, it could be because nobody wants to pay for it. A segment with
2-3 competitors but customer dissatisfaction is often SAFER than a segment with zero
competitors. Competition validates demand.

**Scoring guidance:** The ideal score is 6-8 — existing but beatable competition. Segments
scoring 9-10 require extra scrutiny on Pain Urgency and WTP to confirm the market is real.

## Dimension 5: Founder-Market Fit (Does THIS founder have an edge in THIS segment?)

The core question: Why is this specific founder better positioned to win this specific
segment than a well-funded competitor?

| Score | Description | Evidence Markers |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| 9-10  | Deep insider. Lived experience + network + credibility. | Founder worked in this segment for 5+ years. Has direct relationships with potential buyers. Is known and trusted. Can get 10 meetings in 2 weeks through warm introductions. |
| 7-8   | Strong domain knowledge or strong network in this segment. | Either deep expertise OR warm connections, but not both. Has spoken at segment events or published thought leadership. Former customer of products serving this segment. |
| 5-6   | Adjacent expertise. | Worked in a related segment. Understands the broader industry but not this specific niche. Can learn quickly but starts without insider access. |
| 3-4   | Outsider with transferable skills. | No segment experience. Relies on general startup skills and research. Would need to build credibility from scratch. |
| 1-2   | No connection to this segment. | No domain knowledge, no network, no cultural familiarity. Would be competing against people who have all three. |

**Critical scoring nuance:** Founder-Market Fit is segment-specific, not market-general.
A founder with 10 years in fintech might be a 9/10 for "compliance teams at mid-market
banks" but a 4/10 for "treasury teams at Fortune 500 companies" — same industry, very
different buyer access.

**For first-time founders:** Low FMF does not mean "don't pick this segment." It means the
founder must budget extra time for relationship building and should consider partnering with
a domain expert. Factor this into the 30-day plan.

## Dimension 6: Word-of-Mouth Density (Do customers in this segment talk to each other?)

The core question: If you delight 3 customers in this segment, will 10 more hear about it
without you spending a dollar on marketing?

This is one of Bill Aulet's three defining conditions for a beachhead market, and Geoffrey
Moore's central insight about crossing the chasm: pragmatists buy based on references from
peers they trust.

| Score | Description | Evidence Markers |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| 9-10  | Tight-knit community with active knowledge sharing. | Active Slack/Discord/WhatsApp groups. Regular meetups. Strong industry associations. Buyers explicitly ask peers for tool recommendations. LinkedIn engagement is high within the segment. |
| 7-8   | Moderate community with some knowledge sharing. | Annual conference with networking. Some online forums. Buyers know peers at other companies. Tool recommendations happen but not systematically. |
| 5-6   | Loose professional connections. | Buyers in this segment are aware of each other but don't actively share tool recommendations. Information flows through trade publications more than peer conversations. |
| 3-4   | Isolated buyers. | Companies in this segment operate independently. Little inter-company communication. Word of mouth is slow and unreliable. |
| 1-2   | No community infrastructure. | Buyers have no natural way to learn about each other's tool choices. Each sale is an island. |

**Why this dimension matters more than most founders think:** Customer acquisition cost
(CAC) in a high-WOM segment can be 3-5x lower than in a low-WOM segment. A single
delighted customer in a tight community can generate 5-10 inbound inquiries. In a low-WOM
segment, every customer is acquired independently.

**The counter-argument:** High word-of-mouth also means bad news travels fast. A poor
onboarding experience or product failure in a tight-knit community can damage your
reputation faster than in a dispersed market. This is a feature, not a bug — it forces
product quality — but founders should be aware.

**Data to search for:** Community size (number of members in relevant Slack groups),
conference attendance numbers, LinkedIn engagement rates for segment-specific content,
existence of "best tools" lists shared within the community.

## Dimension 7: Bowling Pin Adjacency (Where does this beachhead lead?)

The core question: After dominating this segment, how many adjacent segments can you
expand into — and how easily?

This is Geoffrey Moore's core strategic insight: your beachhead is not your final market,
it's your launchpad. A beachhead with no adjacency is a dead-end niche.

| Score | Description | Evidence Markers |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| 9-10  | 3+ clear adjacent segments. Each connected by shared buyer role, use case, or channel. | Dominating this segment creates reference customers, product capabilities, and channel relationships that directly transfer to adjacent markets. Think: "CFOs at fintech" → "CFOs at healthtech" → "CFOs at edtech" (same buyer role, different industries). |
| 7-8   | 2 clear adjacent segments with strong connections. | At least one expansion path is obvious. May require some product adaptation but the core value proposition transfers. |
| 5-6   | 1 adjacent segment, or multiple segments with weak connections. | Expansion path exists but requires significant new investment (new features, new channels, new sales motion). |
| 3-4   | Weak or uncertain adjacency. | This segment is somewhat isolated. Expansion would feel like starting a new company rather than extending a beachhead. |
| 1-2   | Dead-end niche. | No natural expansion path. This segment is unique enough that winning it doesn't help you win anything else. |

**Three types of adjacency (in order of strength):**

1. **Buyer-role adjacency** (strongest): Same decision-maker role across different industries.
   "VP Engineering at mid-market SaaS" → "VP Engineering at mid-market fintech."
   The buyer persona, pain points, and purchase process are nearly identical.

2. **Use-case adjacency** (moderate): Same core use case but different buyer or context.
   "CI/CD optimization for backend teams" → "CI/CD optimization for mobile teams."
   The product transfers but the channel and positioning need updating.

3. **Industry adjacency** (weakest): Same industry but different buyer or use case.
   "Compliance for mid-market banks" → "Risk management for mid-market banks."
   Requires new product capabilities but leverages existing relationships.

**Counter-argument to adjacency obsession:** Some founders over-optimize for adjacency
and under-optimize for near-term winnability. A dead-end niche that generates $2M ARR in
12 months is often better than a strategic beachhead that takes 24 months to crack. Cash
flow funds future optionality. Score adjacency but don't let it override a segment that
scores highest on dimensions 1-6.

## Calculating the Overall Beachhead Score

**Simple sum** of all 7 dimensions, displayed as X/70.

**Critical Gap Rule:** Flag any dimension that scores below 4 as a **Critical Gap** regardless
of the overall score. A segment scoring 55/70 with a 2/10 on Market Accessibility has a
fundamental problem that the average obscures.

**Interpretation bands:**

| Overall Score | Verdict | Guidance |
|--------------|---------|----------|
| 55 - 70 | 🎯 Strong beachhead candidate | High confidence. Proceed to 30-day entry plan. The sprint should confirm, not discover. |
| 42 - 54 | ✅ Good candidate with gaps | Solid potential but 1-2 dimensions need attention. The 30-day plan should specifically address weak dimensions. |
| 28 - 41 | ⚠️ Marginal candidate | Significant concerns. Compare carefully against alternatives before committing. Consider whether segment definition needs sharpening. |
| Below 28 | ❌ Weak candidate | Multiple critical gaps. Do not recommend as beachhead unless all alternatives score worse and the founder has a clear plan to address the gaps. |

## Comparison Protocol

When comparing multiple segments, don't just compare total scores. Apply these rules:

1. **Eliminate any segment with 2+ Critical Gaps** (scores below 4) — unless all segments
   have critical gaps, in which case the founder needs to reconsider their segment list.

2. **If two segments are within 5 points:** Look at Dimension 5 (Founder-Market Fit) as
   the tiebreaker. The segment where the founder has stronger access will almost always
   win in practice, regardless of the other dimensions.

3. **If a lower-scoring segment has significantly higher Bowling Pin Adjacency:**
   Present this as a strategic trade-off. "Segment A is easier to win but leads nowhere.
   Segment B is harder but opens three adjacent markets. This is a cash flow vs. strategic
   positioning decision." Let the founder decide.

4. **If the founder is bootstrapped:** Weight dimensions 1-3 (Pain Urgency, WTP, Accessibility)
   higher. Cash flow speed matters more than strategic positioning when you're funding growth
   from revenue.

5. **If the founder is venture-backed:** Weight dimensions 6-7 (Word-of-Mouth, Adjacency)
   higher. VCs care about expansion potential and the story of how Pin 1 leads to a large
   market.

## The "Sharp Enough?" Test

Before finalizing any beachhead recommendation, run this test:

**Can the founder answer all of these questions about the recommended segment?**

1. Name 10 specific companies that fit this segment. _(If they can't, the segment is too broad.)_
2. Name the job title of the person who would buy this. _(If it's vague, the segment lacks
   buyer clarity.)_
3. Describe where 50 of these buyers gather — online or offline. _(If they can't, the segment
   is unreachable.)_
4. State the price this buyer would pay and why it's justified by ROI. _(If they can't, WTP
   is unvalidated.)_
5. Name the #1 tool or workaround these buyers currently use. _(If they can't, competitive
   understanding is insufficient.)_

If the founder can't answer 3+ of these questions, the segment is not sharp enough.
Go back and narrow it further.
