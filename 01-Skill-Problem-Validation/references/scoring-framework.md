# Problem Validation Scoring Framework

## Detailed Rubric for the 6-Dimension Scorecard

This reference file contains the detailed scoring rubric used in Phase 3 of the Problem
Validation skill. Read this before producing the Full Validation Scorecard.

---

## Dimension 1: Problem Severity (How painful is this?)

The core question: If you took away the customer's current workaround, how loud would
they scream?

| Score | Description | Evidence Markers |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| 9-10  | Business-critical pain. Causes revenue loss, compliance risk, or operational failure. | Customer quotes using words like "nightmare," "bleeding money," "keeping me up at night." Active budget allocation to solve. Executive-level visibility. |
| 7-8   | Significant pain. Measurable productivity loss or cost overhead. | Dedicated headcount working around the problem. Quantifiable time/money waste. Mentioned in planning meetings. |
| 5-6   | Moderate pain. Annoying but manageable. | Customers acknowledge it when asked but don't volunteer it. Workarounds exist and are "good enough." |
| 3-4   | Mild inconvenience. | Customers shrug when asked. Low priority. "Yeah, that's a thing, but..." |
| 1-2   | Not perceived as a problem. | Customers don't recognize the problem or disagree that it exists. |

**Key question to ask yourself:** Would a VP or Director at the target company approve a
$30K+ annual budget to solve this? If yes, it's a 7+. If they'd need to be "educated"
about why it matters, it's a 5 or below.

---

## Dimension 2: Problem Frequency (How often does the target encounter this?)

The core question: Is this a daily operational pain or a once-a-year annoyance?

| Score | Description | Evidence Markers |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| 9-10  | Daily or continuous. Part of the core workflow. | The problem is encountered every working day or is always-on. |
| 7-8   | Weekly. Regular enough to be a persistent annoyance. | Comes up multiple times per week. |
| 5-6   | Monthly. Periodic but predictable. | Monthly reporting, quarterly reviews, seasonal triggers. |
| 3-4   | Quarterly or less. Infrequent. | Only during specific events (audits, migrations, annual reviews). |
| 1-2   | Rare or one-time. | Once-in-a-lifecycle events. Hard to build recurring revenue around. |

**Why this matters:** Frequency drives engagement and retention. A daily problem creates
habitual usage (high retention). A quarterly problem requires episodic re-engagement
(harder to build a SaaS business around, but can work for high-value transactions).

**Compound score:** Severity × Frequency is the real signal. A severity-9, frequency-9
problem is a slam dunk. A severity-9, frequency-2 problem can work but requires a
different business model (consulting, project-based, or high-ticket transactions).

---

## Dimension 3: Existing Spend (Are customers already paying to solve this?)

The core question: Is there already money flowing toward solutions in this problem space?

| Score | Description | Evidence Markers |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| 9-10  | Large, established budget category. | Customers have dedicated budget line items. Multiple funded competitors exist. Market size reports quantify the spend. |
| 7-8   | Moderate spend, fragmented. | Customers pay for partial solutions, use multiple tools, or have hired people as workarounds. |
| 5-6   | Some spend, mostly indirect. | Budget is hidden in adjacent categories (consulting, custom dev, manual labor). |
| 3-4   | Minimal direct spend. | Free tools, open-source solutions, or DIY approaches dominate. |
| 1-2   | No evidence of spend. | Customers don't allocate resources to this problem. |

**Critical insight:** Existing spend is the strongest single indicator of a viable market.
If customers are already paying — even for bad solutions — the problem is validated. Your
job becomes "build something better," not "convince people this matters."

**Red flag:** If there's no existing spend AND no workaround behavior, the problem may not
be real enough to monetize. This doesn't mean the idea is dead, but it means the founder
is in "market creation" territory, which requires 10x more capital and time.

---

## Dimension 4: Willingness to Pay (Would they switch to or pay for a new solution?)

The core question: Even if the problem is real, will customers actually open their wallets
for YOUR solution?

| Score | Description | Evidence Markers |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| 9-10  | Demonstrated WTP. Pre-orders, LOIs, or verbal commitments. | "Shut up and take my money" signals. Customers have explicitly said they'd pay. |
| 7-8   | Strong inferred WTP. | Currently paying for inferior alternatives. Switching costs are low. Price point is reasonable relative to pain. |
| 5-6   | Moderate WTP. Conditional. | "I'd consider it if..." responses. Price sensitivity is high. Need to prove ROI first. |
| 3-4   | Weak WTP. | "Interesting, but I wouldn't pay for that." Expect free or low-cost solutions. |
| 1-2   | No WTP evidence. | Market expects free solutions. Open-source dominates. No purchasing authority for this category. |

**B2B-specific considerations:**
- B2B WTP is driven by ROI math. If you can show "this saves your team 10 hours/week at
  $75/hour loaded cost," the $500/month price tag sells itself.
- WTP varies dramatically by company size. A $500/month tool is noise for a 500-person
  company but a real budget decision for a 10-person startup.
- Champion ≠ buyer. The person who loves your product may not control the budget. Score
  based on whether the BUYER would pay, not just the user.

---

## Dimension 5: Market Accessibility (Can you actually reach these customers?)

The core question: Even if the market is real, can this founder efficiently find and sell
to these customers?

| Score | Description | Evidence Markers |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| 9-10  | Highly concentrated and reachable. | Clear channels exist (industry events, specific LinkedIn groups, niche communities, vertical marketplaces). Buyers self-identify. |
| 7-8   | Reachable with effort. | Identifiable companies/titles, but requires outbound effort. Content marketing or partnerships could work. |
| 5-6   | Moderately difficult. | Dispersed across industries. No single channel reaches the majority. Longer sales cycles. |
| 3-4   | Fragmented and hard to reach. | Very small businesses, informal markets, low digital presence. High CAC expected. |
| 1-2   | Practically unreachable at scale. | No clear acquisition channel. Regulatory barriers to outreach. |

**Emerging market modifier:** If the founder is targeting customers in markets with:
- Low credit card penetration → subtract 1 point (payment friction)
- Limited digital presence → subtract 1 point (discovery friction)
- Different business culture around software purchasing → subtract 1 point
- But strong WhatsApp/mobile-first behavior → add 1 point (alternative channel)

---

## Dimension 6: Founder-Market Fit (Does THIS founder have an edge?)

The core question: Why is this specific person better positioned than anyone else to
solve this problem?

| Score | Description | Evidence Markers |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| 9-10  | Deep domain expertise + network + lived experience. | Founder worked in the industry for 5+ years, has relationships with potential buyers, has personally experienced the problem. |
| 7-8   | Strong domain knowledge or strong network. | Either deep expertise OR a strong network, but not both. Relevant technical skills. |
| 5-6   | Adjacent experience. | Related industry, transferable skills, or strong technical capability but limited domain knowledge. |
| 3-4   | Learning the space. | New to the domain. Relying primarily on research rather than experience. |
| 1-2   | No apparent connection. | No domain knowledge, no network, no relevant experience. |

**Why this matters for B2B:** In B2B, founder-market fit is a leading indicator of success.
A founder with 10 years in fintech who builds a compliance tool has a massive advantage over
someone who read about fintech compliance in a blog post. They know the language, they know
the buyers, and they have warm introductions.

**Important nuance:** A low founder-market fit score does NOT mean "don't build this." It
means the founder needs to compensate: hire a domain expert, partner with an industry
insider, or spend significant time in discovery before building. Flag this in the
recommendation.

## Calculating the Overall Score

**Simple average** of all 6 dimensions, displayed as X.X/10.

But also flag any dimension that scores below 4 as a **Critical Gap** regardless of the
overall average. A problem can score 8/10 overall but have a 2/10 on Market Accessibility,
which is a deal-breaker that the average obscures.

**Interpretation bands:**

| Overall Score | Verdict | Guidance |
|--------------|---------|----------|
| 8.0 - 10.0 | 🟢 GO | Strong validation. Proceed to TAM-SAM-SOM with confidence. The 2-week sprint should confirm, not discover. |
| 6.0 - 7.9 | 🟢 GO (with conditions) | Promising but needs sharpening. The 2-week sprint is critical. Flag the 1-2 dimensions that need evidence. |
| 5.0 - 5.9 | 🟡 PIVOT | Core insight may be sound, but the current framing isn't strong enough. Suggest specific pivots: different customer segment, different problem angle, different price point. |
| 3.0 - 4.9 | 🔴 KILL (or major pivot) | Significant concerns across multiple dimensions. Recommend redirecting energy unless the founder has extraordinary conviction AND a plan to address the gaps. |
| Below 3.0 | 🔴 KILL | No evidence supports this problem-solution hypothesis. Be clear, be kind, and suggest where to redirect. |

## The Falsifiable Hypotheses Framework

Each hypothesis must follow this structure:

**"We believe [specific persona] will [specific action] because [specific reason].
We will know this is true when [measurable signal within 2 weeks]."**

Example:
"We believe VP Engineering at Series B SaaS companies (50-200 employees) will book a
demo call within 3 days of receiving our cold outreach because their current CI/CD
pipeline wastes 15+ developer-hours per week. We will know this is true when at least
3 out of 15 outreach targets respond positively."

**Rules for good hypotheses:**
- Must be falsifiable — there must be a clear way to prove it wrong
- Must be testable in 2 weeks — no hypotheses that require 6 months of data
- Must be specific — "people will like this" is not a hypothesis
- Must include a measurable threshold — "at least 3 out of 15" not "some"

Generate 5 hypotheses that cover different risk areas:
1. Problem existence: Does the target actually experience this pain?
2. Problem priority: Is it painful enough to take action on?
3. Willingness to pay: Would they pay [specific price] for a solution?
4. Reachability: Can we get in front of these customers through [channel]?
5. Differentiation: Would they choose our approach over [existing alternative]?

## Kill Signals and Success Signals

### Kill Signals (Stop if you see 2+ of these)
Generate 5 specific kill signals tailored to the founder's situation. Generic examples:
1. "Fewer than 2 out of 10 outreach targets respond to problem-focused messaging"
2. "Customers describe the problem as 'not that big a deal' or 'we've got it handled'"
3. "No customer can articulate what they currently spend (time or money) on this problem"
4. "Every conversation pivots to a DIFFERENT problem as higher priority"
5. "Customers expect this to be free or included in existing tooling"

### Success Signals (Keep going if you see 3+ of these)
Generate 5 specific success signals tailored to the founder's situation. Generic examples:
1. "Customers use emotional language about the problem ('nightmare,' 'hate it,' 'costs us')"
2. "At least 1 customer asks 'When can I buy this?' or 'Can I be a beta tester?'"
3. "Customers can quantify the cost of the problem (hours, dollars, missed revenue)"
4. "Multiple customers describe the same trigger event for the problem"
5. "At least 1 customer offers to introduce you to others with the same problem"
