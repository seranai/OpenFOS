# Value Proposition Canvas — Framework Reference

## Origins & Context

The Value Proposition Canvas was developed by Alex Osterwalder, Yves Pigneur, Greg
Bernarda, and Alan Smith (Strategyzer). It zooms into two core blocks of the Business
Model Canvas: **Value Propositions** and **Customer Segments**.

The canvas is a synthesis tool, not a research tool. It structures what you've learned
from customer discovery — it cannot generate insight on its own. Everything in the
canvas should be grounded in evidence, not assumption.

---

## The Two Sides

### RIGHT SIDE: Customer Profile

Describes a specific customer segment — not customers in general.

**Customer Jobs**
What the customer is trying to accomplish. Three types:
- **Functional jobs**: Specific tasks or problems ("file my quarterly taxes", "reduce
  server downtime", "onboard a new employee")
- **Social jobs**: How the customer wants to be perceived ("look like an innovator to
  my CTO", "be seen as a strategic partner, not just a vendor")
- **Emotional jobs**: How the customer wants to feel ("feel confident in my decisions",
  "stop worrying about compliance risk")

*Key distinction:* Jobs are context-dependent. The same customer has different jobs in
different situations. Always anchor jobs to a specific trigger or context.

**Pains**
Anything that prevents the customer from getting the job done, or makes the experience
worse than it needs to be. Three categories:
- **Undesired outcomes**: Bad results, problems, or characteristics ("the report takes
  4 hours to produce manually", "errors slip through the review process")
- **Obstacles**: Things that prevent the customer from starting or completing a job
  ("we can't afford enterprise software", "our IT team blocks new tool adoption")
- **Risks**: What could go wrong ("what if we switch vendors and lose all our data?",
  "what if this tool gets deprecated?")

*Ranking principle:* Not all pains are equal. Rank by intensity and frequency. A
severe pain that happens daily is far more monetizable than a mild pain that happens
quarterly.

**Gains**
The outcomes and benefits the customer wants. Four levels:
- **Required gains**: Without these, the solution doesn't work ("it must integrate with
  Salesforce")
- **Expected gains**: What a decent solution should provide ("it should be faster than
  what I do today")
- **Desired gains**: What would genuinely impress them ("it proactively flags issues
  before I ask")
- **Unexpected gains**: What would delight them but they'd never think to ask for
  ("it automatically formatted everything for my CFO's preferred reporting style")

*Key insight:* Most products compete on required and expected gains. Winning products
create desired or unexpected gains — that's where loyalty and word-of-mouth live.

---

### LEFT SIDE: Value Map

Describes how your offering creates value for the customer.

**Products & Services**
The tangible things you offer. Be specific — not just "a SaaS platform" but "a web app
with a Slack integration, automated weekly reports, and a mobile alerts feature." This
is the foundation everything else maps to.

**Pain Relievers**
How your products and services relieve specific customer pains. Effective pain relievers:
- Produce savings (time, money, effort)
- Make the customer feel better (reduce anxiety, frustration, hassle)
- Fix underperforming solutions ("we do what [alternative] does but without the 4-hour
  manual export")
- End problems customers face ("no more false positive alerts")
- Eliminate risks ("SOC 2 compliant, so your IT team will approve it")

*Key rule:* You don't need to relieve every pain. Focus on the pains that matter most
to the customer — severe, frequent, and currently unsolved.

**Gain Creators**
How your products and services produce the gains customers want. Effective gain creators:
- Produce outcomes and benefits customers expect or desire
- Outperform existing alternatives in ways that matter
- Make the customer's job easier
- Create positive social consequences ("your team will look proactive, not reactive")
- Fulfill dreams ("imagine walking into your board meeting with this on slide 2")

*Key rule:* Don't try to create every possible gain. Focus on gains that are relevant,
meaningful, and that your product can genuinely deliver.

## The Three Levels of Fit

**1. Problem-Solution Fit**
You've identified real, significant customer pains and gains, and you have a clear idea
of how your product addresses them. Evidence: customer interviews confirm the pain
ranking. The canvas is internally consistent. *Still hypothetical — not yet validated
in market.*

**2. Product-Market Fit**
Customers are actually experiencing the value you designed. Evidence: retention, word
of mouth, unsolicited referrals, customers who would be "very disappointed" if the
product disappeared (Sean Ellis test: >40% = strong signal). *Real, observed behavior.*

**3. Business Model Fit**
The value proposition is also economically sustainable — customers pay enough, often
enough, that the business can grow. Evidence: positive unit economics, improving LTV:CAC
ratio. *Financial viability confirmed.*

Most founders using this skill are at Level 1 or moving toward Level 2.

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Designing for an imaginary customer**
The most common failure. The canvas looks complete but is filled with assumptions, not
evidence. Fix: every pain and gain should be traceable to a customer conversation, a
data point, or a competitive signal.

**Mistake 2: Trying to address all pains and create all gains**
This leads to feature bloat and positioning that says everything and means nothing. Fix:
pick the 2-3 most critical pains and the 1-2 most desired gains and go deep on those.

**Mistake 3: Confusing features with value**
"AI-powered" is not a gain creator. "Reduces time-to-decision from 3 days to 20 minutes"
is. Fix: always translate features into outcomes the customer experiences.

**Mistake 4: Skipping the Customer Profile entirely**
Teams jump straight to the Value Map (their product) without deeply mapping the customer
side first. Fix: spend at least as much time on the Customer Profile as the Value Map.

**Mistake 5: Building the canvas in a room without customers**
The canvas is only as good as the customer insight feeding it. Fix: at minimum, run 5
customer interviews before treating the canvas as validated.

## The Fit Scoring Rubric

Use this when scoring overall fit in Phase 3:

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 9-10 | Every critical pain has a strong reliever. Top desired gains are created. Clear differentiation from alternatives. Customer evidence confirms the mapping. |
| 7-8 | Most critical pains addressed. Key gains created. Some gaps exist but they're in lower-priority areas. Directionally validated by customer input. |
| 5-6 | Core pain addressed but secondary pains ignored. Gains are mostly "expected" not "desired." Product has features that don't map to the canvas. |
| 3-4 | Significant gaps between what the product does and what customers need. Multiple critical pains unaddressed. Value prop is generic. |
| 1-2 | No meaningful fit. Product is built around internal assumptions. Customer profile is vague or wrong. Start over with customer discovery. |

## Relationship to Adjacent Frameworks

**Jobs-to-Be-Done (Christensen, Ulwick):** The "Customer Jobs" section is JTBD in
canvas form. JTBD adds depth — particularly the concept of *struggling moments* (the
specific context that triggers a job) and *desired outcomes* (the metrics customers use
to measure success). For deeper JTBD work, supplement this canvas with Ulwick's
Outcome-Driven Innovation framework.

**Crossing the Chasm (Moore):** The canvas should be built for a specific beachhead
segment — not for "the market." Moore's insight is that early adopters and mainstream
buyers have different jobs, pains, and gains. Build separate canvases for each if you're
targeting both.

**Business Model Canvas (Osterwalder):** The Value Proposition Canvas zooms into the
"Value Propositions" and "Customer Segments" blocks of the BMC. Once the canvas is
validated, the BMC addresses Channels, Customer Relationships, Revenue Streams, and
the rest of the business model.
